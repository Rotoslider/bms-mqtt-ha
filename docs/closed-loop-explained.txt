Closed-loop battery comms on my shop solar setup — what I did and why

The setup
I run the shop off a Sungold SPH5048P (it's an SRNE board under the hood), with
8 Vatrer 51.2V 100Ah rack batteries in parallel and a string of panels. Each
battery has its own BMS, but they were running dumb — no communication to the
inverter. The inverter was estimating state of charge from voltage alone, and
with lithium that barely works. The number would look fine, then the moment
charging stopped it'd fall off a cliff — like 40% gone in a few minutes —
because lithium voltage sags as soon as you quit pushing current in. The pack
wasn't actually draining, the inverter was just guessing badly.

The idea
The batteries do talk over Bluetooth (that's what the Vatrer phone app reads).
So instead of buying anything, I read every cell from all 8 packs over
Bluetooth and feed that back into the inverter as if it were one big smart
battery. Now the inverter charges based on what the cells are really doing
instead of guessing from voltage. People kept telling me "closed loop is the
way," so that's what this is.

How it's put together
A Raspberry Pi does the work — the same one already running Solar Assistant for
monitoring. It connects to each battery over Bluetooth, one at a time, and pulls
voltage, current, temperature, state of charge, and all 16 cell voltages.

The inverter talks to batteries over RS485 using the Pylontech protocol, which
is one of the battery types it already supports. So I put a second USB-to-RS485
adapter on the Pi, wired into the inverter's battery-comms port, and the Pi
pretends to be a Pylontech battery. The inverter asks it for data a few times a
second and the Pi answers with the combined numbers from all 8 packs.

What the Pi actually tells the inverter
It boils the 8 packs down to one virtual battery — averages the voltage, adds up
the current, and then looks at the single worst cell and worst temperature
across all 128 cells. From that it decides the charge limits it hands the
inverter: target voltage 55.2V, up to 50A charge. As the highest cell climbs
past about 3.48V it tells the inverter to ease the charge current down, and it
cuts charging off entirely if any cell reaches 3.60V — which is still below the
battery's own protection trip at 3.75V. Same idea in reverse for a low cell on
discharge. The whole point is the inverter throttles on the weakest cell, so the
strong cells never get shoved too hard while the weak ones catch up. Two of my
packs are a bit out of balance, so this matters.

Keeping it safe
- If the Pi's battery data goes stale, it stops answering on purpose. The
  inverter then falls back to its old voltage-based settings instead of acting
  on bad numbers.
- One-button undo: set the inverter's comms port back to "485" and it's back to
  exactly how it ran before.
- The limits are deliberately conservative, and the Pi only reads from the
  batteries — it never writes any settings to them.
- The monitoring link to Solar Assistant is a completely separate port, so this
  didn't disturb it.

What I'm still chewing on (want your take)
State of charge now comes from the BMS amp-hour counters, averaged across the 8
packs, instead of from voltage. That's a big step up, but those counters only
stay honest if the bank charges all the way full now and then to re-zero. Some
people say I should add a shunt for one accurate bank-level SOC number. Do you
think the BMS average is good enough, or is the shunt worth it? And does the
charge-taper plan (ease off at 3.48V, stop at 3.60V) sound about right to you,
or too cautious? First real test is watching how the SOC drops overnight on just
the standby load.
